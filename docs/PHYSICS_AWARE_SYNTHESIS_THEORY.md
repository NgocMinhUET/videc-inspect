# Physics-Aware Procedural Synthesis for Verification-Driven Underwater Structural Inspection

**Project:** ViDEC-Inspect v0.2
**Document type:** Theoretical and technical foundation
**Scope:** Defines the academic, mathematical, and design basis for the v0.2 synthesis pipeline, replacing the v0.1 placeholder generator. This document does not introduce GAN/diffusion-based components; those are deferred to a later phase as optional label-preserving refinement.

---

## 1. Motivation

Benchmarks for underwater structural inspection must couple visual realism with *verifiable* ground truth. The v0.1 release of ViDEC-Inspect established a validated scaffold with five annotation layers (detection, geometry, metrology, verification, metadata), splits, evaluation, and visualization. However, its image content is produced by a placeholder generator (uniform base color, random circles, mild Gaussian blur), which is sufficient to validate the *pipeline* but inadequate as a scientific image distribution. We outline four reasons why a stronger formulation is required.

**(i) Placeholder textures do not carry inspection-relevant signal.** Inspection algorithms in the underwater domain depend on cues such as edge contrast at crack boundaries, depth discontinuity at spall edges, and color/luminance attenuation due to water. A flat noise field does not contain those cues. Models trained on or evaluated against such images will produce metrics that do not generalize.

**(ii) Underwater inspection is fundamentally an *image formation* problem.** Visibility, color cast, backscatter veiling, and turbidity-dependent blur are not stylistic effects but consequences of the underwater optical channel. A benchmark that does not parameterize these factors cannot expose method sensitivity to operational conditions (water type, lighting, range, sensor degradation), which is the principal axis of evaluation that distinguishes underwater inspection from generic surface inspection.

**(iii) Uncontrolled generative refinement (GAN/diffusion) is unsafe for benchmarks.** Generative models excel at perceptual realism but can silently mutate labeled content: crack skeletons may dilate or break, spall boundaries may smooth or shift, depth-induced parallax may be hallucinated, and exposed-aggregate texture may be invented. When the benchmark is *verification-driven* — i.e., evaluates whether algorithms can decide, defend, and quantify their detections — every pixel touched by an unconstrained generator becomes a potential source of label drift. Treating these models as primary ground-truth generators converts a benchmark into a perceptual study.

**(iv) A physics-aware *procedural* generator preserves exactness.** Procedural synthesis built from interpretable parameters (morphology, material, optics, sensor, task) keeps masks, skeletons, contours, depth maps, and metrology consistent by construction. It also exposes the parameters as ablation axes, which is essential for verification-driven evaluation. Generative refinement may later be layered on top, but only under a *label-preserving* constraint.

We therefore position v0.2 as a *real-informed, physics-aware procedural synthesis pipeline*: not a real-world dataset, not a GAN-based generator, but a controllable model whose distributional choices are motivated by physics and calibrated against real datasets.

## 2. Benchmark environment formulation

ViDEC-Inspect is defined as a benchmark *environment* in which samples are generated by a controllable function rather than collected and post-labeled. Let

\[
z = \{z_{\text{scene}},\ z_{\text{defect}},\ z_{\text{material}},\ z_{\text{water}},\ z_{\text{camera}},\ z_{\text{task}}\}
\]

denote a complete environment configuration, where:

- \(z_{\text{scene}}\): scene family (Phase 1: `flat_wall`), viewpoint, standoff range, surface layout.
- \(z_{\text{defect}}\): per-defect type, morphology, severity (Section 3).
- \(z_{\text{material}}\): substrate appearance — concrete texture, staining, biofouling, coating degradation (Section 4).
- \(z_{\text{water}}\): water type (clear / moderate / turbid), channel-wise attenuation, backscatter strength, turbidity blur (Section 5).
- \(z_{\text{camera}}\): exposure, gain, blur model, noise model, compression (Section 6).
- \(z_{\text{task}}\): the verification requirement — detection, metrology, evidence sufficiency.

The generator is the deterministic map

\[
G(z) \rightarrow \{I_{\text{rgb}},\ D,\ A_{\text{det}},\ A_{\text{geo}},\ A_{\text{met}},\ A_{\text{ver}},\ M\}
\]

where \(I_{\text{rgb}}\) is the underwater RGB observation, \(D\) the metric depth map, and \(A_{*}\) are the five annotation layers introduced in v0.1. Determinism is enforced by seeded sampling of \(z\) and seeded application of stochastic operators.

This formulation enables four properties that an image-only dataset cannot guarantee:

1. **Controllable environment generation** — every axis of \(z\) is independently selectable, supporting matched-pair ablations (e.g., identical scene under three water types).
2. **Exact annotation** — masks, skeletons, contours, and depth modifications are computed in the same coordinate system as the rendered image, so labels are not approximations.
3. **Reproducible evaluation** — the entire sample is a function of `(seed, z)`; benchmark runs are bit-reproducible.
4. **Interpretable ablation** — verification metrics can be conditioned on physically meaningful axes (turbidity, sharpness, range) rather than on opaque embeddings.

## 3. Defect morphology model

We define morphology distributions per class, sampled from priors that are *interpretable, physically motivated, and empirically calibratable*. We explicitly avoid claiming that the model reproduces real defect statistics exactly; instead, parameters are exposed for calibration against real datasets (Section 8).

### 3.1 Crack

A crack is described by:

| Parameter | Symbol | Description |
|---|---|---|
| length | \(L\) | total skeleton arclength (m) |
| mean width | \(\bar w\) | average aperture along skeleton (mm) |
| max width | \(w_{\max}\) | peak aperture (mm) |
| width profile | \(w(s)\) | aperture sampled along normalized arclength \(s\in[0,1]\) |
| tortuosity | \(\tau\) | ratio of arclength to chord length |
| orientation | \(\theta\) | dominant orientation (deg) |
| branch probability | \(p_b\) | probability of branch event per node |
| branch count | \(n_b\) | number of branches sampled from \(\mathrm{Bin}(\cdot, p_b)\) |
| edge roughness | \(\rho_e\) | high-frequency boundary perturbation amplitude |
| local contrast | \(c\) | foreground/background luminance ratio |
| severity | \(\sigma\) | derived from \(\bar w\) via v0.1 thresholds |

Width along arclength is modelled as
\(w(s) = \bar w \cdot \big(1 + \alpha \cdot \mathcal{N}_{\text{smooth}}(s)\big)\),
with \(\alpha\) controlling profile irregularity and \(\mathcal{N}_{\text{smooth}}\) a band-limited Gaussian process. Tortuosity is enforced by walking the skeleton with curvature drawn from a heavy-tailed distribution.

### 3.2 Spall

| Parameter | Description |
|---|---|
| area \(A\) | mask area (m²) |
| perimeter \(P\) | contour length (m) |
| eccentricity \(e\) | ratio of minor to major axis |
| boundary irregularity \(\iota\) | perturbation amplitude on polar radius |
| depth \(d\) | maximum spall depth (mm) |
| depth bowl profile \(d(r)\) | depth as function of normalized radial coordinate |
| edge chipping probability \(p_c\) | probability of small notches along boundary |
| internal roughness \(\rho_i\) | high-frequency texture inside spall |
| exposed aggregate/rebar | Boolean flag and density |
| severity | derived from \(d\) via v0.1 thresholds |

The depth bowl is parameterized as a smoothed paraboloid
\(d(r) = d \cdot \max(0, 1 - r^{p})^{q}\) with shape exponents \((p,q)\) controlling whether the spall is sharp-edged (\(p>2\), \(q<1\)) or shallow-bowl shaped.

### 3.3 Hard negatives and confusables

For each negative type (stain, shadow, texture_variation, biological_growth, surface_artifact):

| Parameter | Description |
|---|---|
| coverage area | spatial extent (m²) |
| edge softness | Gaussian boundary falloff |
| texture frequency | dominant frequency band |
| color shift | hue/value offset from substrate |
| visual similarity to real defects | qualitative pairing (`confusable_with`) |
| confusability score | numeric estimate in \([0,1]\) |

These are *not* required to fool a real detector; they are required to populate the verification space with samples where rejection requires multi-cue reasoning (e.g., a stain with crack-like elongation must be rejected because it has no width-profile topology).

### 3.4 Sampling sources

Parameter distributions are sampled from three sources, in decreasing order of authority:

1. **Engineering priors** — width thresholds from ACI / fib bulletin guidance; depth thresholds from typical reinforced-concrete spall classifications.
2. **Empirical statistics** — moments extracted from real datasets (e.g., crack length distributions in publicly available concrete crack imagery); these inform the *shape* of distributions in the YAML configs.
3. **Expert-defined severity thresholds** — the v0.1 thresholds (1 mm, 4 mm for cracks; 5 mm, 20 mm for spalls) are retained for cross-version comparability.

## 4. Structural material and surface model

The same defect morphology can look radically different depending on the substrate. The material model exposes:

- **Concrete texture** — multiscale noise (low-frequency cement bed + mid-frequency aggregate + high-frequency micro-texture).
- **Roughness** — local luminance perturbation amplitude.
- **Staining** — slow-varying chromatic bias (rust, leaching, organic films).
- **Biofouling** — sparse green/brown patches with soft edges, optionally with mild geometric relief.
- **Coating degradation** — flaking patterns and patchy reflectance.
- **Wet surface reflectance** — specular sheen toward viewing direction.
- **Surface color variability** — per-scene base hue sampled within a calibrated band.

Material parameters are sampled before defects are composited so that defect contrast is meaningful relative to a *non-trivial* background.

## 5. Underwater optical image formation model

We adopt the simplified Jaffe–McGlamery-style image formation model:

\[
I_c(x) = J_c(x)\, e^{-\beta_c d(x)} + B_c\big(1 - e^{-\beta_c d(x)}\big) + \eta_c(x),
\]

with channel index \(c \in \{R,G,B\}\), pixel \(x\), where:

- \(J_c(x)\) is the *clean* surface radiance (output of the material and defect compositing stages).
- \(\beta_c\) is the channel-wise volumetric attenuation coefficient (m\(^{-1}\)); red attenuates fastest, blue slowest.
- \(d(x)\) is the metric range to the scene point (from the depth map \(D\)).
- \(B_c\) is the veiling/backscatter radiance, asymptotic background color as range grows.
- \(\eta_c(x)\) is sensor and environmental noise.

The first term encodes direct attenuation (loss of signal with range), the second encodes backscatter (additive haze from suspended particles), and their interplay produces the characteristic underwater contrast loss and color cast.

Additional effects layered on top:

- **Turbidity-dependent blur** — Gaussian blur with \(\sigma\) sampled from a water-type-conditioned range, approximating multiple-scattering.
- **Contrast scaling** — global linear contrast factor, water-type dependent.
- **Lighting falloff** — radial falloff when artificial light is the dominant illuminant (modelled multiplicatively on \(J\)).
- **Distance dependence** — both attenuation and backscatter are explicit functions of \(d(x)\); near-field structures retain color, far-field structures fade to \(B_c\).

Water types parameterize \((\beta_c, B_c, \sigma_{\text{blur}}, \text{contrast scale})\):

| Water type | \(\beta_{\text{RGB}}\) (m\(^{-1}\)) | Backscatter | Blur \(\sigma\) | Contrast |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| clear    | (0.15, 0.08, 0.04) | 0.02–0.05 | 0.0–0.6 | 0.85–1.00 |
| moderate | (0.35, 0.18, 0.10) | 0.06–0.14 | 0.6–1.5 | 0.60–0.85 |
| turbid   | (0.65, 0.35, 0.22) | 0.15–0.30 | 1.5–3.0 | 0.35–0.65 |

These ranges are intentionally bracketed rather than point estimates, because they are *priors* and must be tunable against real measurements.

## 6. Camera and sensor degradation model

The camera degradation stage applies, in order:

1. **Exposure** — global multiplicative gain on linear intensity.
2. **Gain** — additional pre-noise amplification.
3. **Noise** — Gaussian read noise plus shot-noise approximation; for higher gains, a Poisson-like component dominates.
4. **Motion blur** — anisotropic 1-D kernel along the motion axis when applicable.
5. **Defocus blur** — isotropic Gaussian kernel, parameterized by \(\sigma_{\text{focus}}\).
6. **Compression artifacts** — optional JPEG round-trip at sampled quality.
7. **Optional housing distortion** — mild radial distortion to mimic dome ports.

The order matters: noise is injected *after* gain but *before* compression so that the JPEG step produces realistic structured artifacts in noisy regions. These parameters together govern inspection quality and verification reliability: a sharp, well-exposed image at low gain has high evidence value; a noisy, motion-blurred frame at high gain may force `requires_closeup`.

## 7. Verification-aware quality model

The benchmark's verification layer reasons about whether visual evidence is sufficient to confirm a detection. v0.2 makes that reasoning quantitative by computing per-frame quality metrics from the synthesis stages and propagating them into the ambiguity score that drives verification status.

**Frame quality metrics**

| Metric | Definition |
|---|---|
| `contrast_score` | global contrast retention vs. pre-attenuation reference, mapped to \([0,1]\) |
| `sharpness_score` | normalized variance of Laplacian, mapped to \([0,1]\) |
| `visibility_score` | composite of contrast and color-cast (lower is worse) |
| `color_cast_strength` | distance of channel means from neutral baseline |
| `depth_consistency_score` | from v0.1 depth quality utility |
| `defect_background_separation` | local Δ between defect mask and a dilated background ring |

**Propagation to verification**

- Low `contrast_score` → +ambiguity (defect may be invisible).
- High `color_cast_strength` → +ambiguity for color-dependent cues.
- Low `depth_consistency_score` around a spall → +uncertainty in 3D metrology.
- Low `sharpness_score` → +requires_closeup.
- High `defect_background_separation` → −ambiguity.

The mapping is a transparent additive form, preserving v0.1's `_compute_ambiguity_score` semantics while injecting physical content. Thresholds for `verification_status ∈ {confirmed, uncertain, needs_closeup}` are inherited from v0.1.

This is the central scientific contribution of v0.2 to the verification axis: ambiguity is no longer a function of `difficulty` alone but a function of *what the camera actually saw*.

## 8. Relationship to real datasets

Real datasets are essential, but not as *replacements* for the generator. Their role in ViDEC-Inspect is calibration and validation:

- **Defect morphology statistics** — empirical distributions of crack lengths, widths, and tortuosities; spall area and eccentricity histograms. These calibrate the priors in `crack_model.yaml` and `spall_model.yaml`.
- **Texture/color distributions** — wet-concrete reflectance, biofouling chromaticity, stain hue ranges. These calibrate `material_model.py`.
- **Underwater appearance parameters** — channel-wise attenuation coefficients and backscatter ranges per Jerlov-like water class. These calibrate `underwater_optics.yaml`.
- **Realism validation** — qualitative side-by-side panels and quantitative distribution matching of low-level statistics (contrast, color cast, sharpness histograms).
- **Severity and ambiguity priors** — frequencies of difficult inspection conditions in operational footage inform sampling probabilities.

Real datasets cannot replace the generator because they typically do not provide:

- per-pixel metric **depth** aligned with RGB,
- **exact masks/skeletons/contours** for all defects,
- **metrology labels** (width profiles in mm, depths in mm, volumes in cm³),
- **verification labels** (minimal evidence sets, ambiguity zones, closeup requirements).

Useful sources for calibration include ULTIR (underwater structural inspection imagery), LIACI (long-term underwater hull inspection), UIEB and EUVP (underwater image enhancement benchmarks for color/turbidity priors), and Sea-thru (for explicit attenuation/backscatter estimation). v0.2 does not republish or redistribute any of these; their role is solely to inform prior ranges.

### 8.1 Calibration protocol (v0.2.1 addendum)

The reusable calibration framework lives in `src/calibration/` and is exposed
through `scripts/calibrate_priors.py`. It produces *sibling* configs
(`configs/synthesis/underwater_optics.calibrated.yaml`,
`configs/morphology/{crack,spall}_model.calibrated.yaml`) plus a provenance
JSON, so the v0.2 engineering priors are always recoverable and every
calibrated prior is auditable through a `_field_source` map.

**Optics estimator.** For each image we compute the dark-channel-prior (He
et al.) estimate of the veiling light \(B\), then invert the simplified
image formation equation under an assumed representative range \(\bar d\)
and reference reflectance \(J\):

\[
t_c = \frac{B_c - \mu_c}{B_c - J_c}, \qquad \beta_c = -\frac{\ln t_c}{\bar d},
\]

valid only when \(0 < t_c < 1\); otherwise \(\beta_c\) is reported as
unidentifiable and the corresponding YAML field falls back to the v0.2
prior. Per-corpus aggregation reports the 10th–90th percentile range; auto
water-type binning is supported when explicit labels are unavailable.

**Morphology estimator.** Crack masks are skeletonized to recover length,
mean width (area/length), max width (distance-transform), tortuosity, and
orientation. Spall masks are processed by contour analysis to recover area,
perimeter, eccentricity, and a boundary irregularity index. Lognormal MLE is
fit per metric via `scipy.stats.lognorm.fit` with `floc=0`.

**Identifiability caveats.** Without per-image depth, \(\beta_c\) is
identifiable only up to \(\bar d\); the output is therefore a *prior range*,
not a measurement. Depth-aware calibration (Sea-thru style least-squares fit
on \(\log(B_c - I_c) = \log(B_c - J_c) - \beta_c\,d\)) is recommended when
depth is available. Both modes are first-class in the calibration protocol
documented in `docs/CALIBRATION_PROTOCOL.md`.

## 9. Position relative to GAN/diffusion

The v0.2 generator is *not* a GAN or a diffusion model, and we make this design choice explicit. GAN/diffusion outputs are not adopted as primary ground truth because:

1. They can move, smooth, or eliminate fine defect structure (sub-pixel cracks, sharp spall edges).
2. They do not preserve the depth map, breaking spall metrology.
3. They do not provide controllable severity, water type, or sensor-degradation axes.

In a later phase, a generative model may be introduced as a *label-preserving refinement* operator \(R\): given the procedural output \((I, D, A)\), \(R\) produces a refined \(\tilde I\) with the explicit constraints that

- the mask \(A_{\text{det}}\) is unchanged,
- the depth map \(D\) is unchanged,
- the metrology \(A_{\text{met}}\) is unchanged,
- the verification metadata \(A_{\text{ver}}\) is recomputed from \(\tilde I\) but uses the same ground-truth defect structure.

Until those constraints can be enforced and audited, generative refinement is out of scope. The v0.2 positioning is therefore: *"Physics-aware synthesis provides controllable ground truth; generative refinement may later improve visual realism."*

## 10. Expected contribution

The v0.2 synthesis model contributes to the ViDEC-Inspect benchmark in five ways:

1. **An interpretable generative formulation** for underwater structural inspection benchmarks, expressed as \(G(z)\) over physically meaningful axes.
2. **Defect morphology priors** for cracks, spalls, and hard negatives, with severity and difficulty thresholds aligned across modules.
3. **A simplified but physically motivated underwater image formation model** combining attenuation, backscatter, turbidity blur, and lighting falloff, parameterized by water type.
4. **Verification-aware image quality modeling** that propagates physical quality metrics into ambiguity and verification status.
5. **A reproducible benchmark generation pipeline** that preserves exact annotations (masks, skeletons, contours, depth, metrology, verification) by construction, enabling matched-pair ablations across water, lighting, and sensor conditions.

We position these contributions as scientifically grounded and empirically calibratable, while acknowledging — in the spirit of academic honesty — that the underwater image formation model is a deliberate simplification of full multi-scattering radiative transfer, and that morphology priors are based on standard engineering literature rather than exhaustive empirical estimation. v0.2 is a *real-informed, physics-aware procedural synthesis pipeline*, not a real-world dataset and not a generative-model output.
